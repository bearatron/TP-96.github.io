Who I Am

I'm an aspiring engineer from the Greater Toronto Area, currently in my first year at McMaster University. From a young age I've been drawn to how things work — why planes stay in the air, how forces interact, what happens when a design fails and what it takes to make it not fail again.

My goal is to contribute meaningfully to the future of airline transportation. Whether that means improving the efficiency of existing aircraft, designing new propulsion systems, or rethinking how aviation infrastructure works — I want to be part of that work.

I believe the simplest solution is often the best solution — a principle reinforced through every prototype iteration and project setback I've learned from.

Outside of engineering coursework, I gravitate toward hands-on creation. Machining a mallet on a lathe, building a wooden box with finger lap joints, designing a claw end-effector for a robot arm — these aren't just projects, they're how I've learned to think.

What I Value

01
Simplicity First

Every extra part, variable, or assumption is a potential failure point. The most elegant solution does the most with the least.

02
Build to Learn

Physical prototypes reveal things that no amount of sketching or simulation can. Iterate fast, iterate cheap, and let the thing tell you what's wrong.

03
Design for People

Technology only matters when it solves a real problem for a real person. Every constraint is an opportunity to understand your user better.
